What exactly is SSL anyways?

SSL, or Secure Sockets Layer, is a communications protocol that creates a secure, private, encrypted connection between you and the website you visit. There is a 'handshake' between the website and your computer to verify and authenticate each side. Then, it encrypts the connection so no one else can see what's going on. This allows sensitive information, such as credit card details or payments, to be sent safely over the internet.

OK, but what about TLS?

TLS, or Transport Layer Security, is actually a replacement for SSL. The two terms are so closely related that they are often used interchangeably. The main difference is that TLS does more, such as encrypting other communications like email, messages, and voice. On top of that, TLS is more secure and kept up to date.

How do I know that my page is secure?

There are two ways to verify that a website is secure. First, look at the top of your browser, where the address bar is. There should be a lock icon somewhere. Every browser shows this a little differently, but it should be there. You can click on this lock to verify its authenticity. Second, check the URL of the page you are visiting. Before the 'www.', you should see 'HTTPS://' instead of 'HTTP://'. If both of these are present, the website you're visiting should be secure.

What does "secure" mean anyways?

By basic definition of the word, it means for things to be safe. In terms of browser security, it means that any information that you input is hidden and goes exactly where it supposed to, so that you don't have to worry about it being stolen.

What is encryption anyways and why should I care?

Encryption converts information into a code, making it hard to read unless you have the right key to decrypt it. It locks the information so that only the designated system can open it. If sensitive information isn't encrypted before sending, anyone could see it. Encrypting this information is important to prevent your private, valuable details from being stolen in transit. Imagine writing your credit card information on a piece of paper and throwing it in the air. That's what it would be like if it wasn't encrypted.

Does it matter if HTTP or HTTPS are used when talking about secure sites?

The prefix in every website URL indicates whether or not it is secure. The 'S' at the end of 'HTTP' stands for 'secure,' so if it is not present, the website you are visiting is not secure. You cannot simply add an 'S' to 'HTTP' to make a website secure. A different connection is needed from the server to make it secure.

What is an SSL certificate authority?

A certificate authority (CA) is a company that issues SSL certificates, allowing you to secure your website. This certificate provides authentication, encryption, and integrity. It lets people visiting your website know that you are secure and can be trusted with sensitive information.
